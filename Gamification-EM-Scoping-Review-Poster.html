Gamification Implementation and Outcomes in Emergency Medicine Undergraduate and Postgraduate Medical Training: A Scoping Review

Student ID: 5519695
Introduction

Emergency Medicine (EM) is a high-stakes environment requiring rapid decision-making. Traditional didactic approaches struggle with shift-based schedules and high clinical workloads.

Gamification: The strategic integration of game attributes (points, leaderboards, badges) to foster motivation and engagement in educational activities.

Rationale: Despite gamification being reviewed in radiology and dermatology, evidence in EM remains fragmented. EM has distinct needs (HALO skills, team-based crisis management, just-in-time training).

A scoping review was chosen to map the breadth of modalities, implementation factors, and reported outcomes across a heterogeneous literature—not to determine effectiveness—and to identify research gaps for future studies.

Discussion

Gamification in EM is associated with high learner satisfaction and preference over traditional teaching. Evidence for knowledge or skill gains remains inconsistent; motivation alone does not guarantee outcomes.

Engagement–learning gap: High engagement and “fun,” but this does not consistently translate into better knowledge or skills. The only RCT found higher engagement in the gamified group but no significant learning gain—engagement alone does not guarantee learning.

Results: Modalities (Obj 1)

Top modalities: Escape Rooms (31%), Quiz/Game-Shows (25%), Board Games (19%); Longitudinal (13%), Simulation (6%), Gamified Flipped (6%).

Educational Outcomes (Obj 3)

Learner satisfaction: Overwhelmingly high (81–100%). Preference: 74–95% prefer gamified formats over traditional lectures.

The engagement–learning gap: Heightened engagement did not consistently translate into improved objective knowledge or skills. The only RCT reported higher engagement in the gamified group but no significant knowledge/skill gain. Some studies showed knowledge gains only for content embedded in the game, not for parallel lecture content.

Conclusions & Implications

Bridge the fun-to-learning gap. Ensure clinical competence is measured. Link game elements to outcomes.

Key message for educators: High satisfaction ≠ better learning. Design with objectives first, align mechanics, and measure knowledge—not only satisfaction.
